The key algebraic fact — [P_r, D_W] ≠ 0
This has not been stated explicitly in any prior answer. It is the crux of everything in Experiment B.

From Question 3: [H, γ5] = 0 (proven — both diagonal in tensor product basis).
From Question 1: [H, e_μ] ≠ 0 for μ = 1,...,4 (the spacetime hopping generators).

Proof: H = −½(σz⊗I₂⊗I₂ + I₂⊗σz⊗I₂ + I₂⊗I₂⊗σz), e₁ = σx⊗I₂⊗I₂.
[H, e₁] = [−½σz⊗I₂⊗I₂ , σx⊗I₂⊗I₂]
= −½[σz, σx]⊗I₂⊗I₂
= −½(2iσy)⊗I₂⊗I₂
= −iσy⊗I₂⊗I₂ ≠ 0
Since P_r is a polynomial in H, and [H, e_μ] ≠ 0, it follows that [P_r, e_μ] ≠ 0. Since D_W contains hopping terms proportional to e_μ:
D_W = (m₀+4r)I − ½Σ_μ [(1−e_μ)⊗U_μ + (1+e_μ)⊗U_μ†]

[P_r, D_W] = −½Σ_μ [P_r, e_μ]⊗(−U_μ + U_μ†) + ... ≠ 0
Physical meaning: D_W maps the r-grade subspace INTO other grade subspaces. Every hop of the quark through the lattice (every application of e_μ) mixes the grade channels. The propagator G = D_W⁻¹ accumulates this mixing with every hop.

The random walk picture — what sets the stationary distribution
Concept Content Implication for grade fractions
Grade mixing per hop Each spacetime hop e_μ changes the H-eigenvalue. From the tensor product: e₁ = σx⊗I₂⊗I₂ acting on |↑⟩₁ gives |↓⟩₁ (and vice versa). This shifts H by ±1 for the first-qubit contribution. So each hop is a ±1 step in the H-eigenvalue space {−3/2, −1/2, +1/2, +3/2}. The propagator performs a random walk on the H-eigenvalue ladder. In the free field the walk is unbiased → stationary distribution = dim(r)/8. In the interacting theory, the gauge links U_μ weight different hop directions differently.
Why the walk is biased by gluons The full hopping term is (1±e_μ)⊗U_μ(x). The color gauge link U_μ(x) acts on the ℂ³ factor. It does NOT directly bias the grade walk. However: in the confined phase, U_μ(x) is far from I₃ → the quark picks up a large phase from traversing the gauge link. This phase is entangled with the spinor indices through the hopping structure — the effective hopping amplitude between H-eigenspaces is gauge-link weighted. In the confined phase (β=4.5): large |U_μ − I₃| → larger effective random-walk step size → more grade mixing per hop. In the deconfined phase (β=6.0): U_μ closer to I₃ → smaller effective step size → grade fractions closer to free-field value.
Stationary distribution at long range At large t (t·m_π ≫ 1), C_π^(r)(t) → Z_π^(r) e^{−m_π t}. The grade fraction f_r(t → ∞) → Z_π^(r)/Z_π, the overlap of the pion ground state with the r-grade subspace. This is NOT dim(r)/8 unless the pion wavefunction is exactly uniformly distributed across grade channels. The pion ground state wavefunction encodes the grade structure. Near the chiral limit, the pion is a Goldstone boson of chiral symmetry breaking. Its grade decomposition is a direct probe of how the gauge vacuum structures the spinor weight across the SU(3) subspaces.

What §6.7–6.8 should say as the prediction. The key algebraic result is [P_r, D_W] ≠ 0 — established by [H, e_μ] ≠ 0 (the spacetime hopping generators do not commute with the Cartan hypercharge). This means every lattice hop mixes H-eigenspaces: the quark propagator performs a random walk in grade space whose stationary distribution is set by the gauge dynamics, not by free-field counting. At heavy mass (many hop steps, small propagator range), the free-field theorem still controls the grade fractions because the gauge-link-driven bias is perturbatively small. At κ → κ_c^ren, the propagator becomes long-range, accumulating many gauge-link-weighted grade-mixing steps, and the stationary distribution can depart from dim(r)/8. The physical prediction is that the confined vacuum (β=4.5) drives the stationary distribution toward the color-triplet channels (f_triplet > 3/4) more strongly than the deconfined vacuum (β=6.0), generating a nonzero phase difference in f_triplet that grows as the chiral limit is approached. This is the grade-decomposed analog of ΔA from Paper 1, but is physically distinct: it measures how the vacuum structures the spinor weight across SU(3) representation subspaces, not just the total pion mass. At heavy mass, the phase difference is consistent with zero at 0.6σ — the null result that must be the baseline against which any chiral-limit signal is compared.
